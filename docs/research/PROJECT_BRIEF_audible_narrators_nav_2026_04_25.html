1
Product Vision

Audible is a digital audiobook and podcast platform owned by Amazon, serving millions of listeners globally with a catalog of audiobooks, Audible Originals, and podcasts. Revenue comes through subscription memberships (Standard plan at $8.99/month after trial) and à-la-carte purchases.

This engagement adds a Narrators discovery path to the Browse navigation and creates the corresponding Narrators browse page. Success: a listener who trusts a narrator's voice can find all their work without knowing individual titles — mirroring the existing Authors discovery flow but centered on the performing voice.

2
Problem Statements
Problem 1 — Missing narrator discovery path
Audiobook listeners frequently choose titles based on the narrator's voice. The current Browse nav has no narrator entry point — forcing users to rely on keyword search or chance encounters with narrator metadata on individual title pages. This is a structural gap in the discovery model.
Problem 2 — "Best of the Year" occupies a slot better served by narrator loyalty
"Best of the Year" is a time-bound curation — relevant once a year at most and stale for most of the calendar. Narrator browsing is a perennial, high-frequency behavior. Replacing it with "Narrators" adds a durable year-round discovery path without removing functionality users depend on.
Browse nav open — Popular Lists column (live page, logged-out, 1440px)
Replace: "Best of the Year" → "Narrators" (Q1 confirmed)
3
Target Users

Primary user: Intermediate-to-experienced Audible subscriber. Has completed 3+ audiobooks. Has developed preference for specific narrators by repeat-purchasing books narrated by the same person. Accesses primarily on desktop, browses during leisure time.

Emotional state at key task: Exploratory — looking for their next listen. Has a narrator name in mind OR a voice-type preference, but no specific title yet. Willing to browse but expects the nav to efficiently surface relevant options.

10
Visual Design Language

Aesthetic stance: Clean, functional, catalog-first. White background, dark text, orange CTAs. Editorial restraint — book covers and narrator names are the visual interest; chrome is minimal.

Surface and tone: White page background (#FFFFFF). No gradients or textures in the content area. Nav panel is white with a subtle triple-layer drop shadow.

Color system: Three-color palette — #010E19 (text/structure), #FFA000 (CTA), #1479CF (links, hover, active states). Filter chips use #1479CF solid for the active state. Column headings in the nav use muted blue #4A6687.

Typography: Single font family. Hierarchy through size and weight only: 48px/700 page H1 → 20px/700 book title H2 → 16px/600 chip labels → 16px/400 nav links → 14px/700 CTA labels → 14px/400 body → 13px/400 metadata.

Components: Pill CTAs (32px radius). Rounded filter chips (8px radius). List view for content results, not grid cards. Book covers are square (~232px) with gently rounded corners.

New elements introduced by this engagement: (1) "Narrators" nav link — identical to adjacent dropdown links. (2) Narrators page search bar — standalone input below H1, above filter chips. (3) Four narrator-specific filter chip categories.

User Tasks

Task 1 — Name-based lookup: User knows a narrator's name and wants to see all audiobooks available from them. They use the search bar at the top of the Narrators page to type the narrator's name directly.

Task 2 — Attribute-based discovery: User doesn't have a specific name in mind but wants to find narrators by genre specialty, voice style, or language. They interact with the filter chip row below the search bar to narrow the results list.

Task 3 — Catalog evaluation: User wants to evaluate a narrator before committing to a title. They scroll the results list and review ratings, book count, genre range, and metadata per list item before choosing.
